Recap: Galactic Morphology

Classification Schema

Edwin Hubble organized galaxies based on their visual appearance into a "tuning fork" diagram:

  • Ellipticals (E): Spherical to oval, containing mostly old stars and little gas.
  • Spirals (S/SB): Disk-shaped with spiral arms and active star formation.
  • Irregulars: Lacking a defined shape, often due to mergers.

Recap: Rotation Curves

The Missing Mass

Observations of spiral galaxies show that stars in the outer disk move faster than predicted by visible matter alone.

The Paradox:

Flat rotation curves imply that mass does not decrease as we move away from the center.

This provides critical evidence for a Dark Matter Halo extending far beyond the visible disk.

The Cosmic Web

Large Scale Structure

Matter is not distributed evenly. It forms a network of filaments and sheets, with vast empty voids in between.

Galaxies and clusters reside at the intersections of these filaments, driven by the gravitational collapse of dark matter.

The Expansion Law

v = H₀d
The Hubble-Lemaître Law

The discovery that a galaxy's recessional velocity is proportional to its distance from us. This proves that the Universe is expanding uniformly at every point.

Space itself is stretching, carrying galaxies away from one another like raisins in a rising loaf of bread.

The 1929 Breakthrough

Hubble's Original Plot

In 1929, Edwin Hubble published a landmark paper showing the relationship between distance and recessional velocity for 24 galaxies.

  • Linear Relation: The graph showed that more distant galaxies recede faster.
  • Historical Error: His value for \( H_0 \) was ~500 km/s/Mpc—nearly 7 times higher than modern values due to miscalibrated distance markers.

Image Credit: Hubble (1929).

The Cosmic Tug-of-War

Opposing Forces

The history of the universe is a competition between two dominant, invisible influences:

  • Dark Matter (Gravity): Acts as the "glue" that attracts matter, clumping galaxies together and slowing expansion.
  • Dark Energy (Repulsion): Acts as the "accelerator," pushing the fabric of space apart at increasing speeds.

"Think of the Big Bang as an initial outward push. Gravity tries to pull the breaks, while Dark Energy stomps on the gas pedal."

Dark Matter: The Brake

Slowing the Expansion

In the early universe, matter density was high enough for gravity to dominate. This Matter-Dominated Era allowed for the formation of stars, galaxies, and large-scale structures.

The Glue of Structure

Without the added gravitational pull of Dark Matter, the high-speed stars in galactic halos would have flown off into intergalactic space long ago.

Dark Energy: The Accelerator

Vacuum Energy

Dark Energy appears to be an inherent property of space itself. As the universe expands and more space is created, the total "pushing" force of Dark Energy increases.

Negative Pressure

Unlike matter, Dark Energy has a strong negative pressure that counteracts gravity on cosmological scales, causing space to stretch faster and faster.

It doesn't "clump" around galaxies like dark matter; it is a smooth, uniform field filling the entire void.

The Transition Era

A Changing Balance

For the first 9 billion years, the universe's expansion was decelerating due to the collective gravity of all matter.

  • ~5 Billion Years Ago: The universe grew so large and diffuse that matter density dropped below a critical point.
  • Late-Time Acceleration: Dark Energy became the dominant force, and the universe began to speed up.

The Dark Sector: Cosmic Inventory

  • Dark Energy (~68%)
  • Dark Matter (~27%)
  • Normal Matter (~5%)

The Invisible Universe: Regular matter—everything we can see, touch, and measure—makes up only about 5% of the universe. This implies that the vast majority of physics is currently driven by forces and substances (Dark Matter and Dark Energy) that we do not fully understand yet.

What does it mean for our laws of physics if 95% of the cosmos remains fundamentally alien to our instruments?

The Ultimate Fate

The Big Freeze

If expansion continues at the current rate, galaxies will recede beyond our "Cosmic Horizon," leaving the Milky Way alone in a cold, dark void.

The Big Rip

If the strength of Dark Energy increases over time, the expansion will eventually become so violent that it rips apart galaxies, stars, and even atoms.

Currently, the Big Freeze is our best-supported prediction.

Physics Sprinkle: Data Probe

01. Recession Speed

Assume the Hubble Constant is:

H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc

If a galaxy cluster is 150 Mpc away, calculate its recessional velocity in km/s.

02. Redshift Logic

A galaxy displays a significant redshift in its spectrum. Explain why this shift is considered Cosmological rather than a standard Doppler effect.

Consider the role of expanding space vs. individual motion through space.

Solution: Recession Speed

Working the Math

We use the linear relation established by Hubble:

v=H0⋅d
  • Substitute: \(v = 70 \times 150\)
  • Result: \(v = 10,500 \text{ km/s}\)

Scale Context:

This velocity is roughly 3.5% of the speed of light. At this distance, the expansion of the universe is a dominant effect over any local "peculiar" motion of the galaxy within its cluster.

Solution: Redshift Nature

The "Fabric" Analogy

Unlike a Doppler shift (caused by an object moving *through* space), Cosmological Redshift is caused by the stretching of space itself.

As a photon travels across the expanding universe, its wavelength is stretched along with the fabric of spacetime, shifting it toward the red end of the spectrum.

  • Doppler: Change in wavelength due to relative velocity between source and observer.
  • Cosmological: Change in wavelength due to the increase in the scale factor of the universe during light travel.
